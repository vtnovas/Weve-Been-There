Getting Started

Once you have planned your route, you are ready to prepare your application. USCIS officers review evidence in a few broad categories to understand who you are, how you qualify, and whether you meet the requirements to move forward.

After exploring the categories below, you'll find a table that organizes everything you may need to submit into forms and five evidence categories. Depending on your relationship and your filing path, some items will apply to you and others will not. That is normal. This table is simply meant to help you confirm what matters for your specific situation.

Think of these categories as lenses USCIS uses to understand your case. When all of them are clearly supported, your application is easier to review and may be less likely to be delayed.

1. Identity & Civil Documents

This evidence answers a basic question: who are the people in this application?

These documents establish legal identity and family ties. They include things like birth certificates, marriage certificates, and certified translations. USCIS uses them to confirm names, dates, and relationships before looking at anything else.

2. Relationship Eligibility

This category shows why you are allowed to apply through this family relationship.

For spouses, this includes proof that the marriage is real and ongoing. For parents or children, it focuses on documents that show a legal parent–child relationship. This evidence helps USCIS understand how you qualify, not how long the process will take or how strong your case "feels."

3. Immigration Status & Entry (Adjustment of Status Only)

This evidence explains how the beneficiary entered the U.S. and what their current status is.

It includes records like passport pages, visas, and arrival documents. USCIS uses this information to confirm that the beneficiary is eligible to apply from inside the U.S. or to understand their immigration history more broadly.

4. Financial Sponsorship

This category answers the question: will the applicant be financially supported?

USCIS requires proof that the petitioner (or a joint sponsor, if needed) meets income requirements. This includes tax returns, proof of employment, and sometimes assets. This is not about wealth. It is about showing basic financial stability as defined by USCIS rules.

5. Admissibility & Background

This evidence helps USCIS confirm that the applicant is eligible to receive a green card under U.S. law.

It includes the immigration medical exam and, in some cases, records related to prior arrests or court cases. Many people never need to submit anything beyond the medical exam in this category.

6. USCIS Forms

The official forms required to process your application.

These are the completed government forms that tie all your evidence together and formally request immigration benefits. Each form serves a specific purpose in your application packet.

Preparing Your Application

The table above helps you understand what USCIS may ask for based on your situation. This section focuses on a different question: how do you put your evidence together in a way that is clear, complete, and easy to review? Preparing your evidence is about making sure you have what you need, and presenting it in a thoughtful, readable manner so your application tells a clear story from start to finish.

Get Your Documents Ready

Let's start with what documents you should have ready that will help you fill out your forms. Be sure to make copies if they need to be submitted for evidence, since you will not get them back. USCIS will specifically ask if they need an original of a certain evidence type.

For the Petitioner (U.S. citizen or green card holder) ▼
  • Proof of status (U.S. passport, birth certificate, naturalization certificate, or green card)
  • Proof of relationship (marriage certificate, birth certificate of child, etc.)
  • Proof of prior divorces (if it applies to you)
For the Beneficiary (Immigrant) ▼
  • Passport biographic page
  • Birth certificate (with certified English translation if in another language)
  • Marriage certificate (if marriage-based)
  • I-94 arrival/departure record (if in the U.S.)
  • Visa used to enter the U.S. (if it applies to you)
  • Medical exam results (Also known as Form I-693, submitted with or after I-485)
  • Prior immigration records (if it applies to you)

The Forms

There are many forms with names that may get confusing. If you have trouble differentiating them, here is each form's name, who files it, and a description to help you understand the differences.

I-130 | Petition for Alien Relative

Filed by the petitioner

This form tells the U.S. government you want to bring a family member to live in the U.S.

I-485 | Adjustment of Status

Filed by the beneficiary (if applying from inside the U.S.)

This form is used to apply for a green card without leaving the U.S.

I-864 | Affidavit of Support

Filed by the petitioner

This form shows that the immigrant has someone who can support them financially.

I-693 | Report of Medical Examination and Vaccination Record

Filled out by the civil surgeon, filed by the beneficiary

This form shows that the immigrant has someone who can support them financially.

I-765 | Application for Work Authorization (Optional)

Filed by the beneficiary (if also submitting I-485)

This form lets the immigrant ask for permission to work while their green card is in process.

I-131 | Application for Travel Document (Optional)

Filed by the beneficiary (if also submitting I-485)

This form is used if the immigrant wants to travel outside the U.S. and return while their case is still pending.

Building Your Packet

Now let's go through each evidence category and prepare your packet to submit with your forms.

1. Identity & Civil Documents

Purpose: Establish who everyone is and how they are legally connected.

This category includes documents like birth certificates, marriage certificates, divorce decrees, and certified translations. These documents form the foundation of your application. USCIS uses them to confirm names, dates, and legal relationships before looking at anything else.

How to prepare and organize

  • Use clear, readable copies. Do not submit originals unless specifically required since they will not send them back.
  • Make sure names and dates are consistent across documents.
  • If a document is not in English, include:
    • A full English translation
    • A signed translator certification
      If you would like to use a template, you can use the Google Drive template linked below:
      Translation Certificate Template
  • Group these documents together at the front of your packet or under a clearly labeled divider like "Identity & Civil Documents."

This section is about accuracy. Clean, legible, and easy to verify is the goal.

2. Relationship Eligibility Evidence

Purpose: Show why your family relationship qualifies under immigration law.

This category looks different depending on the relationship.

For spouses (bona fide marriage) ▼

USCIS is looking for signs of a shared life over time.

What strong evidence looks like:

  • Documents that span different points in time
  • Evidence that shows shared responsibility or decision-making
  • A mix of official records and everyday life proof

How to organize:

  • Group evidence by type or theme. For example:
    • Housing
    • Finances
    • Insurance and benefits
    • Photos
  • Label everything. A simple caption goes a long way.
    • Who is in the photo?
    • When it was taken?
    • Where it was taken?

Avoid submitting dozens of similar items. A small, representative set is usually stronger than volume. Remember, this section answers "why you qualify," not "how much you love each other." Clarity matters more than emotion here.

If you would like a template for organizing and captioning your photos, you can use the Google Drive one using the link below:

Photo Caption Template
For parents or children ▼

This category is more straightforward and focuses on legal proof rather than shared life.

How to organize:

  • Place birth certificates, adoption decrees, or legitimation documents together.
  • If something requires explanation, include a short written note rather than assuming USCIS will infer context.

3. Immigration Status & Entry (Adjustment of Status Only)

Purpose: Explain how the beneficiary entered the U.S. and their current immigration context.

This category applies primarily to Adjustment of Status cases, and covers:

  • How the beneficiary entered the U.S.
  • When the entry occurred
  • Under what status or authorization

How to organize:

  • Start with the passport biographic page.
  • Follow with the visa page used for entry.
  • Include the Form I-94 or entry/parole record.
  • Add any prior immigration documents in chronological order.
  • Keep everything in one section rather than scattering these documents across the packet.

Helpful mindset

This is a timeline, not a test. USCIS wants to quickly understand the sequence of events without needing to dig for it.

4. Financial Sponsorship

Purpose: Show that the applicant will be financially supported as required by law.

This section centers around the Affidavit of Support and its supporting evidence.

If using a joint sponsor or household member, keep their documents grouped together as a complete mini-packet.

How to organize

  • Include the most recent required tax return first.
  • Follow with supporting documents like W-2s, pay stubs, or employer letters.
  • If there are multiple sponsors, don't mix their documents together without headings.

Helpful mindset

This is just about meeting a threshold. Clear numbers and attribution matter here.

5. Admissibility & Background

Purpose: Confirm eligibility to receive a green card under U.S. law.

For many applicants, this category is simple and limited.

What this usually includes:

  • The immigration medical exam (Form I-693)
  • Vaccination review conducted by a civil surgeon

If you have previous criminal charges, this also includes record of those. Include certified court dispositions if there were arrests or charges. Do not submit partial records or summaries, and place explanations directly after the related document if needed.

How to organize:

  • Keep sealed medical exam result sealed.
  • Group any legal records clearly and label them neutrally.

Helpful mindset

This category is often smaller than people expect. If it applies to you, clarity and completeness matter. If it does not, that is normal.

Building Your Packet

Once you have gathered your documents, the final step is putting everything together in a way that is clear and easy to review.

USCIS officers review many cases every day. A well-organized packet does not guarantee approval, but it does reduce confusion and lowers the chance of delays caused by missing or misplaced documents.

Start With Structure

A strong packet usually follows a simple flow:

  1. A short cover letter (optional but helpful)
  2. Payment
  3. Completed forms
  4. Identity and civil documents
  5. Relationship evidence
  6. Financial sponsorship evidence
  7. Immigration history (if applicable)
  8. Medical exam (if included at time of filing)
  9. Any additional supporting documents

You do not need to over-engineer this. The goal is logical sequencing, not perfection.

The Cover Letter

A cover letter in a family-based green card application works like a guide for the immigration officer reviewing your case. It explains who is applying, what forms are included, and what documents are in the packet. Since officers review many applications every day, a clear cover letter helps them understand your case quickly.

The cover letter is not required, but it is a good idea to include one. It shows that your application is organized and complete. It can also explain anything that might cause confusion, such as a past marriage or a name change. Overall, the cover letter helps make your application easier to review and may help prevent delays.

Group by Category, Not by Document Type

Avoid mixing everything together.

For example:

  • Keep all financial documents together.
  • Keep all relationship evidence together.
  • Keep immigration history documents together.

When evidence is scattered, officers have to search for context. When it is grouped, the story becomes clearer.

Keep It Clean and Easy to Scan

  • Use single-sided printing unless instructions say otherwise.
  • Avoid heavy stapling. Binder clips are often safer.
  • Do not bind the packet in a way that makes it hard to separate.
  • Place dividers between major sections if mailing physically.

For digital uploads, use clear file names like:

  • I-130.pdf
  • Marriage-Certificate.pdf
  • Joint-Bank-Statements-2022-2023.pdf

A Final Note

Preparing your evidence is about helping someone unfamiliar with your life understand your situation quickly and clearly.

If your packet is…

  • Logically grouped
  • Clearly labeled
  • Easy to skim

…then you are doing this very well.
